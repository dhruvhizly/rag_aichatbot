The ARI Tug Handling Simulator is a dedicated training platform designed to
develop and assess competency in the handling of tug vessels across a range of
operational and emergency scenarios. It is used to train both novice and
experienced tug operators in precision manoeuvring, ship assist operations,
towage, and emergency response, without risk to personnel, vessels, or port
infrastructure.

SECTION 2: PRODUCT OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The ARI Tug Handling Simulator provides a practical and realistic training
platform for tug operators to improve handling skills and rehearse emergency
manoeuvres. It supports multiple tug configurations, including ASD (Azimuth
Stern Drive) tug types, and accurately reproduces complex tug behaviour under
varying environmental and operational conditions.

The simulator is a specialised training tool designed to bring precision and
safety to one of the most challenging roles in maritime operations. By providing
an authentic recreation of a modern tug's manoeuvring station and navigation
bridge, the simulator allows both novice and experienced operators to hone their
skills without risking expensive assets or endangering port infrastructure.

A core strength of the platform is its sophisticated behavioural model, which
ensures that the physics of tug movements — including the complex dynamics of
ASD tugs — are replicated with extreme accuracy. This allows operators to
experience how different environmental variables, such as wind and current,
affect the vessel's responsiveness during critical manoeuvres, and to develop
the instinctive handling skills required for safe and effective tug operations.

SECTION 3: WHAT IS A TUG AND WHAT DO TUG OPERATORS DO?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

3.1 WHAT IS A TUG?

A tug (tugboat) is a powerful, highly manoeuvrable vessel used to assist larger
ships during berthing, unberthing, and transit through confined waterways. Tugs
are also used for offshore towage, salvage operations, firefighting, and as
emergency response vessels in ports and harbours.

Tugs are among the most technically demanding vessels to operate. Their work
requires precise control in confined spaces, often in close proximity to high-
value ships, quayside structures, and other port infrastructure. Errors in
tug handling can result in serious damage to vessels, port facilities, and risk
to personnel.

3.2 TUG TYPES

Several tug propulsion and hull configurations exist, each with distinct
handling characteristics:

Conventional Tug:
Uses a fixed propeller and rudder arrangement. Manoeuvring is achieved through
propeller thrust and rudder deflection. Less manoeuvrable than ASD or VSP tugs
but widely used in many ports.

ASD Tug (Azimuth Stern Drive):
Fitted with azimuth thrusters at the stern, which can rotate through 360 degrees
to direct thrust in any direction. ASD tugs are highly manoeuvrable and are the
predominant tug type in modern port operations. The ARI simulator specifically
supports ASD tug training.

Tractor Tug (VSP — Voith Schneider Propeller):
Uses Voith Schneider cycloidal propellers fitted forward of amidships, capable
of generating thrust in any direction instantaneously. Highly responsive but
with different handling characteristics to ASD tugs.

Robert Allan / Ship Docking Tug variants:
Various purpose-built configurations for specific port and offshore environments.

3.3 WHAT DO TUG OPERATORS DO?

Tug operators (tug masters) are responsible for:
- Assisting large vessels during berthing and unberthing manoeuvres in port
- Escorting vessels through channels and restricted waterways
- Providing push and pull forces under the direction of the ship's pilot or master
- Connecting and managing towlines during ship-assist and towage operations
- Responding to emergencies including vessel breakdowns, fires, and groundings
- Operating in close proximity to structures and other vessels with precision

The role requires exceptional situational awareness, precise throttle and
thruster control, and the ability to anticipate how the tug and the assisted
vessel will respond to environmental forces.

SECTION 4: KEY FEATURES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

4.1 DIVERSE VESSEL LIBRARY
The simulator includes multiple tug types, with ASD tugs as a primary
configuration, alongside other tug variants for varied training. The diverse
vessel library allows training programmes to cover the handling characteristics
of different propulsion systems and hull forms, ensuring operators are
competent across the range of vessels they may be required to operate.

ASD tug training within the simulator covers:
- Azimuth thruster control and thrust vectoring
- Forward and indirect (indirect mode) towing techniques unique to ASD tugs
- The effects of towing force on ASD tug stability and manoeuvring
- Transitioning between push and pull modes during ship-assist operations

4.2 SOPHISTICATED BEHAVIOURAL MODEL
The simulator employs a high-accuracy behavioural model that replicates the
complex physics of tug movements under varying conditions. The model accounts for:
- Hydrodynamic forces acting on the tug hull
- Propeller and thruster thrust vectors and interaction effects
- Towline tension, angle, and catenary behaviour
- Wind forces on hull and superstructure
- Current and tidal flow effects
- Interaction effects between the tug and the assisted vessel
- Shallow water and bank effects in confined waterways

This level of fidelity means that the responses trainees experience in the
simulator closely mirror those they will encounter on a real tug, making
skills developed in training directly transferable to operational duties.

4.3 REALISTIC LAYOUT
The simulator closely resembles modern tug manoeuvring stations and navigation
bridges, including realistic representations of:
- Tug propulsion control levers and thruster joysticks
- Navigation displays and instruments
- Communication systems
- Fendering and towing equipment controls
- External view from the tug bridge, replicating sightlines and visual cues
  used by tug operators during manoeuvres

The authentic layout reduces the gap between simulator training and real
operational environments, allowing operators to transfer skills directly without
needing to relearn control configurations.

4.4 EMERGENCY RESPONSE TRAINING
The simulator includes dedicated scenarios for practising critical emergency
manoeuvres. Emergency training scenarios cover:
- Emergency towing of a disabled vessel with loss of propulsion
- Fire-fighting support operations
- Vessel grounding response and refloating assistance
- Man overboard response in a port or harbour environment
- Collision avoidance and emergency breakaway from an assisted vessel
- Tug capsize avoidance — managing girting and tripping risks when towing
- Sudden environmental changes (squalls, current surges) during critical operations

Emergency scenario training ensures operators develop the decision-making speed
and muscle memory required to respond safely under pressure.

4.5 CONFIGURATION FLEXIBILITY
The simulator is available in multiple configurations to suit different training
needs and institutional requirements. Configuration options allow training
establishments to:
- Select the tug types and propulsion systems relevant to their trainees
- Scale the simulator setup from single-station to multi-station environments
- Integrate the tug simulator within broader port or ship manoeuvring simulation
  exercises
- Customise the training environment to reflect specific ports, channels, and
  operational areas relevant to the trainees' workplace

SECTION 5: TRAINING CAPABILITIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

5.1 TARGET AUDIENCE
The ARI Tug Handling Simulator is designed for tug personnel at all levels,
including:
- Trainee tug operators and deckhand personnel in initial tug familiarisation
- Junior tug masters developing precision handling skills
- Experienced tug masters rehearsing complex or infrequent manoeuvres
- Tug operators transitioning to new tug types, particularly ASD tugs
- Port authority and pilotage personnel involved in ship-assist coordination
- Maritime training instructors delivering tug handling courses

5.2 BASIC HANDLING SKILLS TRAINING
Foundational tug handling competencies developed include:
- Understanding and controlling ASD thruster vectors
- Low-speed manoeuvring in confined spaces
- Approaching and connecting to an assisted vessel
- Maintaining position and applying controlled push or pull forces
- Responding to helm and engine orders from a ship's pilot or master
- Safe berthing and unberthing assistance techniques

5.3 ADVANCED MANOEUVRING TRAINING
Advanced handling skills developed include:
- High-load towing operations and towline management
- Indirect towing mode operation unique to ASD tugs
- Escort tug operations in open water and channel transit
- Multi-tug coordination in complex ship-assist scenarios
- Manoeuvring in strong wind and current conditions
- Operating in shallow water and alongside quay structures

5.4 EMERGENCY RESPONSE TRAINING
Crisis management and emergency response competencies include:
- Emergency towing procedures for disabled vessels
- Girting and tripping avoidance — critical safety skills for tug operators
- Emergency breakaway procedures when a tow becomes unmanageable
- Coordination with port emergency services and MRCC during incidents
- Maintaining tug stability during high-load emergency operations

5.5 ENVIRONMENTAL CONDITION TRAINING
The simulator exposes trainees to a full range of environmental conditions:
- Varying wind speeds and directions
- Tidal streams and river currents
- Shallow water effects on tug handling
- Bank cushion and bank suction effects in narrow channels
- Restricted visibility and night operations

SECTION 7: SAFETY AND OPERATIONAL CONTEXT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Tug operations carry specific safety risks that the simulator is designed to
address through dedicated training:

Girting (Girthing):
One of the most dangerous hazards in tug operations. Girting occurs when a
towline exerts a transverse force on the tug that causes it to be pulled
broadside and potentially capsized. ASD tugs operating in indirect towing mode
are particularly susceptible. The simulator provides a safe environment for
operators to experience, recognise, and avoid girting situations.

Tripping:
Similar to girting, tripping occurs when a tug's propulsion forces and the
towline tension interact to cause the vessel to heel and potentially capsize.
Understanding and managing tripping risks is a core competency developed in
tug handling training.

These capsize risks mean that tug simulator training is not simply a matter
of convenience — it provides a genuinely safe learning environment for
practising scenarios that would be extremely dangerous to rehearse on a real
tug in live operations.

SECTION 8: COMPLIANCE AND STANDARDS CONTEXT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Tug handling training requirements are referenced within several international
and national maritime frameworks:

STCW Convention:
STCW competency tables for officers in charge of a navigational watch and for
masters and chief mates include manoeuvring and ship handling competencies
relevant to tug operators. The simulator supports training aligned to these
competency requirements.

IMO Model Course 1.22 — Ship Simulator and Bridge Teamwork:
The simulator supports training relevant to IMO Model Course 1.22 where
applied to tug operations and ship-assist scenarios.

Port Authority and National Licensing Requirements:
Many port authorities and national maritime administrations have specific tug
master licensing requirements. The ARI Tug Handling Simulator provides the
training platform to support preparation for these assessments and examinations.

SECTION 11: FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What tug types does the ARI Tug Handling Simulator support?
A: The simulator includes multiple tug types, with ASD (Azimuth Stern Drive)
tugs as the primary configuration. Additional tug variants including conventional
tugs are available, and the platform supports configuration flexibility to match
the vessel types relevant to the trainee's operational role.

Q: What is an ASD tug and why does it require specialised training?
A: An ASD (Azimuth Stern Drive) tug is fitted with azimuth thrusters at the
stern that can rotate 360 degrees to direct thrust in any direction. This gives
ASD tugs exceptional manoeuvrability but also introduces unique handling
characteristics — including indirect towing mode and specific girting and
tripping risks — that require dedicated training to master safely.

Q: What is girting and why is simulator training important for it?
A: Girting (or girthing) is a dangerous situation where a towline exerts a
transverse force on the tug, pulling it broadside and risking capsize. It is
one of the most serious hazards in tug operations, particularly for ASD tugs
in indirect towing mode. The simulator provides a safe environment to experience,
recognise, and avoid girting — a scenario that would be extremely dangerous to
rehearse on a real vessel.

Q: How realistic is the tug handling behaviour in the simulator?
A: The simulator uses a sophisticated behavioural model that replicates complex
tug physics with high accuracy, including hydrodynamic forces, thruster
interactions, towline dynamics, wind and current effects, and vessel interaction
forces. The responses trainees experience closely mirror those on a real tug.

Q: Can the simulator be used for emergency towing training?
A: Yes. The simulator includes dedicated emergency response scenarios covering
emergency towing of disabled vessels, fire-fighting support, grounding response,
man overboard, emergency breakaway procedures, and capsize avoidance in high-
load situations.

Q: What environmental conditions can be simulated?
A: The simulator replicates a full range of environmental conditions including
varying wind speeds and directions, tidal streams and currents, shallow water
effects, bank cushion and bank suction in narrow channels, and restricted
visibility and night operations.

Q: Is the simulator layout similar to a real tug bridge?
A: Yes. The simulator closely resembles modern tug manoeuvring stations and
navigation bridges, including authentic propulsion control levers, thruster
joysticks, navigation instruments, and realistic visual sightlines. This reduces
the adjustment required when trainees transition from simulator training to
real tug operations.

Q: Can the tug simulator be integrated with wider port simulation exercises?
A: Yes. The simulator's configuration flexibility allows it to be integrated
within broader port or ship manoeuvring simulation exercises, enabling multi-
vessel, multi-operator training scenarios that replicate real ship-assist
operations involving tugs, pilots, and the assisted vessel simultaneously.
